Met police bosses order super-sized uniforms with 61-inch waists as blobby bobbies pile on the pound

Posted by Jenniffer Sheldon on Sunday, August 4, 2024

POLICE chiefs have had to order super-sized uniforms with waists up to 61in, shock figures show.

They have bought nine pairs of the giant patrol trousers for men in the past two years.

Met bosses have also paid for three 56in pairs and ­similar-sized summer leathers for a motorbike cop.

In total, 624 pairs of trousers in girths of at least 40ins have been bought this year, on top of 7,796 last year.

It makes a mockery of then-Met supremo Sir Bernard Hogan-Howe’s 2015 warning that overweight officers faced the axe.

He said fit cops needed them in emergencies. He added: “They don’t want somebody waddling down the road who, when they get there, is out of breath.”

The Met shopping list also included three 50in waterproofs for search advisers and dog handlers plus 52in women’s patrol trousers.

Seven pairs of 44in cycling trousers and a gun cop’s 43in combat trousers were also bought, figures from Freedom of Information laws reveal.

Any of the force’s 31,000 officers who need to handcuff or restrain suspects must undergo annual fitness tests.

That involves shuttle runs totalling 525 metres to be completed in 3mins 40secs.

It became compulsory in England and Wales in September 2014 and 1,800 officers failed in the first year.

In 2013 The Sun told how Met Sgt Andy Sharp, then Britain’s tubbiest officer, controlled crowds at a demo and a Help for Heroes bike ride.

Yesterday, National Obesity Forum chief Tam Fry said: “At any time officers may be called on to perform exceptional duties.

"Chiefs should ensure they are fit for purpose and have the time and facilities to keep in shape.”

NHS experts warn men should lose weight if their waists hit 37in and women if they reach 31½in.

Men with girths over 40in and women over 34in face very high rates of heart disease, Type 2 diabetes and stroke risk.

The Met said yesterday: “All job-related fitness tests must be set to reflect the physical demands of the role.

“Where officers fail, they are given development plans and support to help them meet the standard.”

In 2012 a quiz of Met cops and staff showed two in three overweight or obese.
