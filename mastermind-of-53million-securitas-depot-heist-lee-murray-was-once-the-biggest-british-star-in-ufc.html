Mastermind of 53million Securitas depot heist Lee Murray could have been the biggest British star

Posted by Aldo Pusey on Monday, May 27, 2024

THE Securitas depot heist remains the biggest cash robbery in British history with £53MILLION stolen by a gang of armed raiders in February 2006.

The "mastermind" behind the job, according to other members of the group, was Lee Murray, who is now serving 25 years in a Moroccan prison.

But what many don't know is that Murray was also one of the best cage fighters ever produced in Great Britain - although he never got the chance to prove it.

He'd grown up in and around Woolwich, South East London, and throughout his teenage years was connected to organised crime with his gang, the Barney Boys (named after the Barnfield Estate).

Murray spent time in Feltham Young Offender Institution for selling marijuana and crack-cocaine and is said to have been in charge of a network of drug runners.

A veteran of hundreds of street fights, Murray turned to organised brawling in the late 1990s.

"He's a scary son of a b****... and I don't mean fighter-wise."

UFC President Dana White on Lee Murray

After winning his first fight - a quick knockout that earned him the nickname Lightning - Murray began to take his training more seriously.

He trained at London Shootfighters and Peacock's boxing gym, before travelling to the US to train under the famous Pat Miletich.

Robbie Lawler, a former UFC welterweight champion, remembers Murray well from their time together at the gym having frequently sparred with him.

He told Sports Illustrated: "Murray had world class punching power.

"Man, he would hit the mitts—pop-pop-POP-POP and you would stop your workout and look over because it sounded like gunfire."

Murray eventually returned to the UK and more success in the cage.

Standing at 6ft 3in but able to cut his weight to middleweight, Murray  was a ferocious puncher who had vastly improved his ground game.

Murray also lived something of a playboy lifestyle, driving a yellow Ferrari while being linked to a string of Page 3 girls.

He was headed for big things, but a string of incidents saw him leave the fight game.

The UFC will be in London on Saturday night, with 20,000 fans packing into the O2 Arena to see Jimi Manuwa and Leon Edwards at Fight Night.

UFC, Bamma, Bellator and other promotions have made MMA one of the fastest-growing sports in the world.

Millions of fans tune in to watch and big brands pay huge money to sponsor fighters, TV coverage and support the different strands.

Saturday will be the ninth time the UFC has staged a show in London - but the first time was very different.

Back then, British MMA was the Wild West, with events organised and frequented by London's criminal underbelly.

Murray was at the first event as a spectator... and was involved in one of the most infamous nights in the promotion's history.

After the event (aptly named the 'Brawl in the Hall') a number of UFC fighters and executives headed to a local nightclub, a time-honoured tradition.

At closing, Murray and friends were outside with Miletich and a few others, while Tito Ortiz, the most dangerous man in the UFC at that time, was also there.

There was a misunderstanding and Paul Allen - who would later be involved in the Securitas raid and had the nickname 'The Enforcer' - punched a friend of Ortiz.

This sparked an almighty street fight that has gone down as part of UFC lore.

Eye-witnesses claim that Ortiz - the UFC light-heavyweight at the time - and Murray were backed into a corner and squared up.

Tito, who would have stood two stone heavier than Murray, swung for Murray, but the Londoner made him miss and then fired off a rapid combination that decked the bigger man.

Murray was still making his name in MMA but became the thing of legend.

President Dana White would later say of Murray: "He's a scary son of a b****... and I don't mean fighter-wise."

Murray continued his climb in the sport and eventually made his impressive UFC debut in January 2004, earning a submission win over Jorge Rivera.

He entered the ring wearing an orange boiler suit and a Hannibal Lecter mask.

Nine months later the Londoner pushed Brazilian legend Anderson Silva all the way, losing a narrow decision to a fighter who would go on to be a poster boy for the company.

The defeat to Silva would prove to be his last fight... inside a cage at least.

In the months following the fight, Murray was convicted of committing grievous bodily harm in a road rage attack.

He was also arrested and questioned twice in the murder of Sabina Rizvi, who was shot dead in Bexleyheath after a row over a car.

Another man, Paul Asbury, was later jailed for life for the murder, but police believe others were involved.

In September 2005, while training for a fight at Wembley Arena, Murray was involved in another street brawl - but this one had much more severe consequences.

He had been at Funky Buddha for the birthday bash of Lauren Pope, the Page 3 girl turned Towie star, and was stabbed multiple times.

Murray explained in a 2005 interview with MMAweekly: "One of my friends got involved in the fight. I tried to help him because about six or seven guys was on [him].

"I got stabbed in the head first... I thought it was a punch... I just wiped the blood and continued to fight.

"I looked down at my chest and blood was literally shooting out of my chest... It was literally flying out of my chest like a yard in front of me... I died three times."

A week earlier he had also been at Funky Buddha - and got in a fight where his nipple was slashed off.

It signalled the end of his time in MMA.

Promoters in the UK were unwilling to book him, his fights frequently saw crowd trouble, while he was not allowed to travel to the US as he couldn't get a visa after his GBH conviction.

There was said to have been a $78,000 contract on the table from the UFC.

Five months after that stabbing, Murray would lead a team to a Securitas depot in Tonbridge, Kent, and make away with £53,116,760.

"This was a brilliant caper which turned into a farce."

Howard Sounes, author of Heist

On the evening of February 21, 2006, depot manager Colin Dixon was pulled over on the A249 by what he thought was an unmarked police car.

The car had flashing blue lights and two men in the front wearing hi-vis police uniforms.

A man approached his vehicle and even flashed a Kent police badge - but was wearing a prosthetic mask.

At the same time, Mr Dixon's wife and eight-year-old son were being held hostage at their home in Herne Bay, Kent.

They had answered the door to men also dressed in fake police uniforms.

Both Mr Dixon and his family were then driven to a farm in Staplehurst, where they were told at gunpoint that failure to co-operate could lead to them being harmed.

The armed group then travelled to the depot, where 14 members of staff were bound by the balaclava-clad gang.

Armed with handguns, shotguns, skorpion machine pistols and AK-47 assault rifles, the gang moved with military precision.

Staff were then left locked in cash cages, while cash was stuffed into the back of a lorry.

One member of the gang, Ermir Hysenaj, had been the classic inside man - working while wearing a small video camera hidden in his belt buckle.

Murray was believed by police to be the robber nicknamed "Stopwatch".

He was seen on CCTV tape bossing the gang around and timing their every move while wearing an electronic stopwatch around his neck.

The heist became international news overnight, with massive rewards offered to anyone who had knowledge of the crime.

Four days after the raid, Murray and Allen fled to Morocco.

But the gang did plenty to speed up their own demise.

Within ten days, five people had been charged and millions of pounds had already been recovered.

Murray is said to have provided a key piece of evidence for the prosecution himself.

In the days before the job he crashed his yellow Ferrari, leaving a mobile phone in the vehicle.

A recording of him talking about the robbery with a co-conspirator was recovered and later used by prosecutors.

A make-up artist who had made prosthetic masks for the robbers, later turned, testifying against the gang.

She was later reported to have a £7million price placed on her head.

Howard Sounes, author of a book on the heist, later said: "This was a brilliant caper which turned into a farce."

While members of the gang were being arrested Murray, along with Paul Allen, fled to Morocco - where Murray's father was from, granting him citizenship.

At the time, Morocco had no extradition treaty with Great Britain, and the pair were able to live a lavish lifestyle with their ill-gotten gains.

He is said to have driven a gold Mercedes-Benz.

Murray spent almost £1million buying and refurbishing a house that included marble floors and a fully equipped gym.

A giant mural depicting his UFC victory was painted on a wall, while he commissioned a statue of himself.

However, unbeknown to the pair, Kent police and Scotland Yard had contacted Moroccan authorities, who put them under 24-hour surveillance.

Both were eventually arrested. Allen was sent back to the UK - and released from prison in 2016 - but Murray was not extradited because of his heritage.

An exchange for suspected terrorist Mohamed Karbouzi was rejected by the Home Office.

In June 2009, Murray attempted to escape from a Moroccan prison, with small saws smuggled inside a plate of biscuits, but another prisoner foiled the plot.

A year later, Lightning Lee Murray was charged and convicted for his part in the Securitas heist.

He was initially sentenced to ten years, to be served inside a Moroccan jail, but that was extended to 25 years in November 2010.

Det Supt Mick Judge said: "I'm pleased Murray will now begin serving a significant prison sentence for his part in the Tonbridge robbery."

Murray, who has reportedly fathered a child while incarcerated, remains in a Moroccan prison.
